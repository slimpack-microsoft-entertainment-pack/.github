<h2>Purpose of Microsoft Entertainment Pack</h2>
<p>Microsoft Entertainment Pack was created by Microsoft as a collection of casual, easy-to-play games for Windows - intended to demonstrate the gaming capabilities of Windows (when most games were DOS-based) and to offer light entertainment suitable even for office users. The pack made it possible to enjoy card games, puzzles, arcade-style games and simple simulation directly under Windows GUI environment. Over time the Entertainment Pack became a part of many users' Windows experience, establishing early traditions of bundled Windows games.</p>

<h2>Main Features & Game Library</h2>
<p>The original Microsoft Entertainment Pack series (four volumes released between 1990 and 1992, plus a "Best of" compilation in 1994) includes a wide variety of games across genres: card games, puzzle games, arcade-style reflex games, simple simulations, and even a screensaver utility.</p>
<ul>
  <li>Classic card games: Solitaire-type games such as FreeCell, Cruel, Golf, Tut's Tomb, and others.</li>
  <li>Puzzle and tile games: Taipei (Mahjong-style), Pegged (peg solitaire), TetraVex, Klotski, Pipe Dream, and more.</li>
  <li>Arcade and reflex games: Classic titles such as Minesweeper, SkiFree, JezzBall, Rodent's Revenge, and chip-collecting platformers like Chip's Challenge.</li>
  <li>Screensaver and casual utilities: The pack also included simple utilities / screensavers (IdleWild) as part of the offering.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Unique Advantages and Historical Significance</h2>
<p>At a time when most PC games required DOS or specialized engines, Microsoft Entertainment Pack showed that Windows - previously considered productivity-only - could be used for games as well. This lowered the barrier for casual gaming, making games accessible on typical Windows PCs without switching operating environments.</p>
<p>Many of the games in the pack - such as Minesweeper, FreeCell, or Taipei (which later evolved into Mahjong Titans) - became staples of the Windows ecosystem and were carried forward into future Windows releases.</p>
